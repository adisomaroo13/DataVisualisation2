Clean Electricity & Coal: Global Patterns

Where in the world is energy getting cleaner? And how did coal unit designs evolve?

What we are analysing

We look at leaders in clean energy (as of 2024) We also analyse clusters where coal is still being used, and how it evolves over the years.

Why it matters

Countries starting clean today can decarbonise faster. Where coal units are newer and larger, retirement or retrofit timelines matter more. Reading these together shows who’s clean and the context i.e what legacy units we built

How to read the visualisation

Start with the map: darker teal means a cleaner power mix. Hover to see exact percentages. The lollipop chart then spotlights the top performers, while the top & bottom scatter shows how the rest of the world spreads out, with a dashed line marking the 50% milestone. Finally, the coal charts explain the “why”: dots and heat cells reveal when and what size coal units were built. Use the slider to focus on bigger units and watch how patterns change by decade.

Coal plant unit size vs commissioning year

Bigger coal units surged through the 1990s to the 2000s. Post-2010, growth cools as renewables rise. Use the slider to focus on mid-to-large units.

Why this pattern?

The rise of large coal units in the 1990s to 2000s mirrors an era of fast-growing demand, cheap capital and a push for steady baseload. Bigger turbines meant better efficiency, so many countries standardized on 300 to 1,000 MW blocks.

After 2010 the curve bends: renewables became cheaper, air quality rules tightened, and financiers raised the cost of coal. Mega projects slowed, new builds shifted smaller or stopped, and retirements began to nibble at the fleet. Outliers often reflect captive industrial plants, late stage completions, or refurbishments.

What this suggests

Systems that built the largest units face longer decarbonisation lead times, while countries with fewer post 2010 additions can pivot faster to clean power without stranding new assets.
